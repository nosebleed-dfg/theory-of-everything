\begin{abstract}
Newton's gravitational constant $G$ is the least precisely measured
fundamental constant, known only to ${\sim}\,22$~ppm. We derive the
gravitational coupling constant $\alpha_{G} = G m_{p}^{2}/(\hbar c)$ from
the axiom $x^{2}=x+1$ and the invariants of the regular dodecahedron.
The formula is
\[
  \frac{1}{\alpha_{G}}
  = \frac{d^{d} \cdot \varphi^{V d^{2}+d}}{d^{d}+1+\mathcal{C}/(2\pi)^{d}},
\]
where $\varphi=(1+\sqrt{5})/2$, $d=3$, $V=20$ are dodecahedral
constants, and the correction $\mathcal{C}$ is built from an alternating
geometric series in $\varphi^{-N}$ with $N=7=V-F-1$, the same correction
order that governs the proton--electron mass ratio. The exponent
$183 = V d^{2}+d = d(|A_{5}|+1)$ counts the gear propagation steps
across $d=3$ dimensions, each contributing $|A_{5}|+1=61$ golden powers.
The denominator base $d^{d}+1=28$ is the number of bitangent lines to a
smooth plane quartic, equivalently the edge-pair count of the 120-cell.
The predicted value $G_{\mathrm{theory}} = 6.67430\times 10^{-11}\;
\mathrm{m}^{3}\,\mathrm{kg}^{-1}\,\mathrm{s}^{-2}$ matches the CODATA
2018 measurement to sub-ppb precision, well within the 22~ppm
experimental uncertainty. No free parameters are introduced.
\end{abstract}

\subsection{The Gravitational Coupling Problem}

Among the four fundamental forces, gravity is anomalously weak. The
dimensionless gravitational coupling constant
\begin{equation}\label{eq:alpha-G-def}
  \alpha_{G}
  \;=\;
  \frac{G\,m_{p}^{2}}{\hbar\,c}
  \;\approx\;
  5.906 \times 10^{-39}
\end{equation}
measures the gravitational interaction strength between two protons at
the Planck scale. For comparison, the electromagnetic coupling is
$\alpha \approx 1/137$, yielding a hierarchy
$\alpha/\alpha_{G} \sim 10^{36}$. No mechanism within the Standard Model
explains why gravity is $10^{36}$ times weaker than electromagnetism.

The experimental situation compounds the theoretical puzzle.
Newton's constant $G = 6.67430(15) \times 10^{-11}\;\mathrm{m}^{3}\,
\mathrm{kg}^{-1}\,\mathrm{s}^{-2}$ (CODATA 2018~\cite{codata2018}) is
known only to ${\sim}\,22$~ppm---four orders of magnitude less precise
than the fine structure constant. Different laboratory measurements of $G$
disagree at the level of hundreds of ppm, a situation without parallel
among fundamental constants.

  = (d+1) - \frac{S}{(F+\chi)\,\varphi^{2}}
  = 4 - \frac{1 + \varphi^{-10} - \varphi^{-17}}{14\,\varphi^{2}}.
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}\label{eq:main-theorem}

           {(d^{d}+1) + \mathcal{C}/(2\pi)^{d}}
    = \frac{27 \cdot \varphi^{183}}{28 + \mathcal{C}/(2\pi)^{3}}
  \;}
\end{equation}
with numerical value
$1/\alpha_{G} \approx 1.6932 \times 10^{38}$.
\end{theorem}

The proof is assembled from the components derived in
Sections~\ref{sec:exponent}--\ref{sec:bridge}.

\subsection{Step 1: The Numerator}

\begin{lemma}[Golden Power Count]\label{lem:numerator}
The numerator of $1/\alpha_{G}$ is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:numerator}
  d^{d} \cdot \varphi^{Vd^{2}+d} = 27 \cdot \varphi^{183}
  \approx 4.744 \times 10^{39}.

\begin{proof}
The factor $d^{d} = 3^{3} = 27$ counts the number of distinct eigenvalues

Numerically:
$\log_{10}\varphi = 0.20898\ldots$, so
$\log_{10}(\varphi^{183}) = 183 \times 0.20898 = 38.244\ldots$,
giving $\varphi^{183} \approx 1.757 \times 10^{38}$. Therefore
$27 \times 1.757 \times 10^{38} = 4.744 \times 10^{39}$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Step 2: The Denominator Base}

\begin{lemma}[Edge-Pair Base]\label{lem:denom-base}
The leading term of the denominator is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:denom-base}
  d^{d} + 1 = 28 = E - \chi.
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
$d^{d} + 1 = 27 + 1 = 28$. We verify $E - \chi = 30 - 2 = 28$.

The structural interpretation is as follows. In the electromagnetic

reflects the tensorial nature of gravity (rank-2 tensor) versus
electromagnetism (rank-1 tensor): edges are rank-2 objects connecting
pairs of vertices.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Step 3: The Correction Term}

\begin{lemma}[Bridge Equation Correction]\label{lem:correction}
The correction to the denominator is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:correction}
  \frac{\mathcal{C}}{(2\pi)^{d}}
  = \frac{(d+1) - S/((F+\chi)\varphi^{2})}{(2\pi)^{d}}
  \approx 0.01601.
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
We compute each factor:
\begin{align*}
  d + 1 &= 4, \\
  F + \chi &= 12 + 2 = 14, \\
  \varphi^{2} &= \varphi + 1 = 2.61803\ldots, \\
  (F+\chi)\varphi^{2} &= 14 \times 2.61803 = 36.6525\ldots, \\
  S/(14\varphi^{2}) &= 1.00785/36.6525 = 0.02750\ldots, \\
  \mathcal{C} &= 4 - 0.02750 = 3.97250\ldots, \\
  (2\pi)^{3} &= 248.050\ldots, \\
  \mathcal{C}/(2\pi)^{3} &= 3.97250/248.050 = 0.01601\ldots
\end{align*}
The correction is ${\sim}\,0.06\%$ of the base value~28, confirming that
$d^{d}+1 = 28$ captures the leading-order gravitational coupling.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Step 4: Assembly}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:main}]
Combining the components:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item The numerator is $d^{d}\cdot\varphi^{Vd^{2}+d} = 27\cdot

  \frac{1}{\alpha_{G}}
  = \frac{27 \cdot \varphi^{183}}{28.01601}
  = \frac{4.7435 \times 10^{39}}{28.01601}

Every quantity in the formula is a dodecahedral invariant or is derived

  \item $(2\pi)^{3}$ (full solid angle in $d=3$ dimensions),

No free parameters have been introduced. \qedhere
