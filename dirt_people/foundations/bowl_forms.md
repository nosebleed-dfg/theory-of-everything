## The Core Idea

The completed L-function Lambda(s) achieves its minimum modulus at sigma = 1/2 — not just on average, but POINTWISE for every height t. The critical line is the bottom of the bowl.

Three independent mechanisms force the same conclusion:

1. **Vieta (the axiom):** x^2 = x + 1 has roots summing to 1. Center = 1/2. This is geometric, not analytic.

2. **Functional equation:** Lambda(s) = epsilon * Lambda(1-s). The reflection axis is sigma = 1/2.

3. **Energy integral (Hadamard three-lines):** Phi(sigma) is log-convex. The functional equation forces Phi(sigma) = Phi(1-sigma). Log-convexity + symmetry = minimum at the axis.

All three agree: sigma = 1/2 is the bottom. This is the bowl.

---

## The Bowl is Pointwise, Not Average

|Lambda(1/2 + it)| <= |Lambda(sigma + it)|  for all sigma, all t.

Every point on the equator is lower than every point off the equator at the same height. This is not a statistical statement. It holds at every t individually.

Zeros of Lambda must occur at minima (where Lambda = 0). A zero off the axis would require the off-axis value to be lower than the on-axis value — contradiction with the bowl structure.

---

## Connection to the Bicone

The bowl minimum at sigma = 1/2 is the same structure as the bicone from the golden axiom:

- The bicone has its "equator" (minimum circumference) at the midpoint
- The L-function has its minimum modulus at sigma = 1/2
- Both are forced by the x^2 = Tx + D axiom where D = 1, K = 1/4 (Koppa quarter-phase)

The bicone IS the bowl. Same geometry, different domain.

---

## What Closes the Proof

The bowl establishes that 1/2 is the unique minimum. The remaining step (GRH gap): showing that off-axis zeros would violate the energy minimum — i.e., that Lambda(s) = 0 off the line is incompatible with the bowl inequality.

This requires connecting the Weil positivity / explicit formula to the bowl. The explicit formula distributes zero contributions as positive energy. If a zero sits off-axis, it contributes asymmetrically to the energy functional and breaks the symmetry that the functional equation guarantees.
